# VocaForm HeyGen Video Plan

**Format:** Hybrid avatar presentation and real product capture<br>
**Final runtime:** 2:27, within the 2:50 production ceiling<br>
**Canvas:** 16:9 landscape, 1920 × 1080<br>
**Export:** 1080p MP4, 30 fps, SDR<br>
**Primary goal:** Make the working product unmistakable while using the avatar to create a human opening and close.

## Recommended HeyGen workflow

Build this in **AI Studio**, not Quick Avatar Video. Use the custom avatar for the opening, the engineering-evidence scene, and the closing. Let the real VocaForm screen recording fill the frame during the product journey so the interface stays readable.

For the final edit, keep the calm visual language but add contrast at the handoffs. Use a 0.4–0.6-second cross-dissolve when moving between the avatar and the application, paired with a gentle 102–105% push toward the destination frame. Use the animated VocaForm logo lockup only for the first avatar-to-product handoff and the move into engineering evidence. Product-to-product cuts should remain clean and direct.

Capture the product as separate clips before final assembly:

1. landing and opening the synthetic medical sample;
2. compiled-form readiness and source evidence;
3. Realtime interview states and saved answers;
4. deterministic review, final verification, and completed PDF;
5. Memory Vault suggestions on the next form;
6. architecture/evidence card.

If the HeyGen plan includes AI Studio Screen Recorder, record with the microphone off and add each take as an editable scene. Otherwise, record externally and upload MP4 or MOV clips through Media. Keep five seconds of clean footage before and after each action so trimming is easy.

For the Realtime section, include one short, audible exchange from the app if the capture reliably records system audio. Make that its own scene with no avatar narration. If the audio is unreliable, keep the product clip muted and show the visible Listening, Thinking, Speaking, and Saving states under narration.

Use the avatar full-frame rather than as a permanent picture-in-picture overlay. VocaForm uses the upper-left, upper-right, central task area, and right status card; there is no consistently safe corner across the journey. A small avatar may appear on the right of the engineering-evidence scene because that scene can be designed around it.

## Scene plan and narration

### Scene 1 — Human problem and promise

**Time:** 0:00–0:17<br>
**Visual:** Custom avatar, waist-up, on a restrained warm-white VocaForm background. Animate the logo and the four words `Upload · Talk · Review · Download` beside the avatar.<br>
**Delivery:** Direct and empathetic, with more urgency than the rest. Add a 0.5-second pause after the pain point, then lift the energy on the promise.

**Narration:**

> Dense forms turn simple tasks into frustrating work. They ask people to decode unfamiliar language, type the same details again, and catch every hidden condition. VocaForm turns that paperwork into one calm conversation—and gives you back a completed document.

#### Accessibility-commercial opening alternative

**Time:** 0:00–0:20<br>
**Visual:** Branded commercial card with the avatar, the headline `Forms should work without relying on sight`, and four proof points: voice guidance, screen-reader structure and live status, full keyboard control, and high-contrast 200% reflow.<br>
**Delivery:** Empathetic and confident. State the barrier directly, lift on the voice-conversation promise, and finish the accessibility proof points with commercial momentum.

> For people who are blind or have low vision, a dense form can feel like a wall. VocaForm turns it into one guided voice conversation. Upload the document, hear one clear question at a time, answer naturally, then review and download—with screen-reader structure, full keyboard control, high contrast, and 200% text reflow built in.

#### Inclusive-commercial opening alternative

**Time:** 0:00–0:22<br>
**Visual:** Keep the accessibility-commercial design language, but broaden the headline to `Dense forms shouldn’t slow anyone down`. Balance general benefits—natural voice answers and one-question-at-a-time guidance—with screen-reader, keyboard, high-contrast, and reflow support.<br>
**Delivery:** Commercial and inclusive. Open on the universal annoyance, acknowledge the greater accessibility barrier without narrowing the audience, and finish on the concrete inclusive features.

> Nobody enjoys fighting through a dense form. Small fields, repeated questions, and visual clutter turn simple tasks into work—and can create real barriers for blind and low-vision users. VocaForm replaces that friction with one guided voice conversation. Upload, listen, answer naturally, review, and download—with screen-reader structure, keyboard control, high contrast, and 200% text reflow built in.

### Scene 2 — Upload and understand

**Time:** 0:22–0:41<br>
**Visual:** Full-screen recording. Open the synthetic medical sample, then hold on the compiled-form summary, field counts, readiness state, and source evidence. Use one subtle cursor highlight on the sample and one on the readiness result.<br>
**Delivery:** Clear and confident. Lead with the avoided manual work, then give the model name room. Lift slightly on “ready for a conversation.”

**Narration:**

> I start with a synthetic medical intake PDF. Instead of mapping every field by hand, VocaForm uses GPT-5.6 Sol to understand what the form asks, what is required, and where every answer belongs. Every question stays tied to source evidence, so unsupported interpretations are blocked before the conversation begins.

### Scene 3 — Talk, with code in control

**Time:** 0:40–1:04<br>
**Visual:** Full-screen recording. Start Voice and show Requesting microphone, Listening, Thinking, Speaking, and Saving. Save several answers, interrupt one model turn, then briefly show the equal Type answer path. Optionally place a four-to-six-second un-narrated live exchange in the middle.<br>
**Delivery:** Calm and explanatory at first, then accelerate slightly as the state moves through Listening, Thinking, Speaking, and Saving. Pause before and emphasize “code—not the model.” Treat the first successfully saved answer as the first “aha” moment.

**Narration:**

> Now OpenAI Realtime guides the interview by voice. Watch the state move from listening, to thinking, to speaking, to saving. Each answer passes through versioned application tools before it becomes part of the form. If speech is interrupted or a call repeats, code—not the model—decides what is stored. There is an equal keyboard path, and a dropped connection resumes from server-owned state.

### Scene 4 — Review, verify, and export

**Time:** 1:04–1:25<br>
**Visual:** Full-screen recording. Enter Review with one visible blocker, correct it, run final verification, and show the passed state. Continue to Download, open the completed PDF, and hold at a legible zoom on populated fields.<br>
**Delivery:** Deliberate. Leave a brief pause after “cannot rewrite the user’s answers,” then lift the energy on the passed state and “eight of eight fields placed.”

**Narration:**

> Before export, VocaForm checks missing, invalid, and dependency-sensitive answers. Then GPT-5.6 Sol runs a separate semantic review. It can point out ambiguity or a contradiction, but it cannot rewrite the user’s answers. When the checks pass, VocaForm fills a copy of the original PDF and reports exact placement coverage. The uploaded source stays unchanged.

### Scene 5 — Consent-based memory

**Time:** 1:25–1:41<br>
**Visual:** Full-screen recording. Show the three approved contact facts, open the school sample, and confirm one suggestion. Keep the provenance text readable.<br>
**Delivery:** Reassuring, not defensive. Add a small note of delight when the three approved suggestions appear on the next form.

**Narration:**

> On the next form, the Memory Vault suggests only contact details I explicitly approved. Nothing is reused until I confirm it. Medical answers are excluded, and every remembered item can be corrected or forgotten.

### Scene 6 — Public web-form hand-off

**Time:** 1:41–1:59<br>
**Visual:** Full-screen recording. Show the inspected public responder form, the prepared provider controls, the final verification, and the provider-labelled Submit button. Keep the on-screen disclosure that nothing has been submitted visible.<br>
**Delivery:** Clear and trustworthy. Emphasize the final user-controlled action.

**Narration:**

> Public Google and Microsoft Forms use the same trusted journey: read-only inspection, voice or text interview, and fresh consent before supported answers are prepared in an isolated provider page. VocaForm verifies every control again, then stops before Submit. The final click stays with the user.

### Scene 7 — Build Week engineering evidence

**Time:** 1:58–2:19<br>
**Visual:** Avatar on the right; a simple evidence card on the left. Show `Modular TypeScript`, `53/53 fields`, `0 fabricated`, `Accessibility + resilience gates`, and a small `Codex + GPT-5.6` label. Avoid a scrolling repository view.<br>
**Delivery:** Warm, confident founder endorsement with continuous natural motion. Proud and more animated than the workflow explanation, while remaining factual.

**Narration:**

> Codex became my build partner. I used it to research APIs, rebuild my prototype in modular TypeScript, and test browser and accessibility flows—even continuing remotely from my phone. Inside VocaForm, I used GPT-5.6 Sol to compile unfamiliar forms and run a separate semantic review. Together, they gave me speed without giving up human control.

### Scene 8 — Close

**Time:** 2:18–2:27<br>
**Visual:** Custom avatar, then a gently animated VocaForm logo lockup, `vocaform-build-week.onrender.com`, and the four-step line. End on a clean still frame for at least one second.<br>
**Delivery:** Punchy, firm, and measured. Use crisp stops, but leave deliberate breathing room after `Download`, `One conversation`, and `Done`. Land `Done` and `made human` with conviction.

**Narration:**

> VocaForm. One form. One conversation. Done.

## HeyGen setup notes

- Use a calm or conversational Voice Director setting for explanatory lines. Switch to a warmer, more energetic direction for the three “aha” moments and the close; do not use a uniformly excited sales delivery.
- Apply one voice to all scenes and preview every scene before rendering the avatar.
- Add 0.5-second script pauses at the marked beats and between major clauses that sound crowded.
- In the HeyGen Brand Glossary, keep the written term `GPT-5.6 Sol` and set its spoken pronunciation to `jee pee tee five point six soul`. Audio-preview every line containing it before generating. If the Dutch custom voice still distorts the phrase, use Voice Mirroring for that complete sentence rather than changing the caption text.
- Add pronunciation rules for `VocaForm`, `Realtime`, `AcroForm`, and `Codex` before generating. Keep all correctly written terms in captions.
- Use 0.4–0.6-second cross-dissolves with a restrained 102–105% push for avatar/application handoffs. Avoid animated template transitions during product actions.
- Add one soft, instrumental background bed: warm ambient texture, light piano or muted pluck, roughly 72–84 BPM, no vocals, no prominent drums, and no dramatic melodic hook. Keep it approximately 18–22 dB below narration, duck it another 4–6 dB around key lines, and remove it entirely during any real Realtime exchange. Fade in over 0.8–1.0 seconds and fade out over the final 1.2 seconds.
- Use only music with an explicit license that permits a public YouTube submission and is unlikely to trigger Content ID. Verify the uploaded video for copyright claims before submission.
- Enable stylized captions, proofread them, and reposition them scene by scene if they cover an active control. Also upload the final SRT separately to YouTube.
- Generate short avatar scenes first. Fix voice, pacing, and pronunciation before spending credits on the final full render.

## Product capture checklist

- Use reviewed synthetic fixtures only.
- Hide notifications, bookmarks, personal tabs, account pages, and terminal history.
- Keep browser zoom and window size consistent across clips.
- Move the cursor only to demonstrate an action; do not circle controls continuously.
- Hold important success states for two to three seconds.
- Show the completed document at a readable zoom.
- Keep the final rendered duration below 2:55 and the published YouTube duration below 3:00.
- Verify the finished video and captions in a signed-out YouTube window at 1080p.
